Western Art History Timeline

Renaissance

Renaissance

14th - 17th Century

Humanism · Perspective · Classical Revival

Emerging in 14th century Florence, the Renaissance marked Europe's rebirth after the Middle Ages. Artists rediscovered Greco-Roman ideals through anatomical accuracy and linear perspective, creating illusionistic depth unseen since antiquity. Patronage shifted from church to wealthy merchants, allowing secular themes to flourish. Da Vinci's Mona Lisa perfected sfumato technique, while Michelangelo's Sistine Chapel fused theology with humanist ideals. The era saw the rise of universal geniuses (uomo universale) mastering multiple disciplines, from Brunelleschi's dome engineering to Alberti's architectural theories.

Key Artists: Leonardo da Vinci, Raphael, Titian

Baroque

Baroque

17th Century

Drama · Chiaroscuro · Movement

Born from Counter-Reformation zeal, Baroque art employed theatricality to evoke emotional devotion. Caravaggio's tenebrism created stark spotlit dramas in works like The Calling of St Matthew, while Bernini's Ecstasy of St Teresa transformed marble into flowing drapery and divine light. Monarchs like Louis XIV used Versailles' Hall of Mirrors as political propaganda. Dutch Golden Age painters like Vermeer elevated genre scenes through meticulous detail and symbolic vanitas elements. The period's dynamic diagonals and swirling compositions, seen in Rubens' Descent from the Cross, aimed to immerse viewers in spiritual ecstasy.

Key Artists: Caravaggio, Rembrandt, Rubens

Romanticism

Romanticism

Late 18th - Early 19th Century

Emotion · Sublime Nature · Individualism

Reacting against Industrial Revolution mechanization, Romantics championed raw emotion and nature's awe-inspiring power. Turner's Slave Ship used turbulent seas to condemn human cruelty, while Friedrich's Wanderer Above the Sea of Fog epitomized existential solitude. Delacroix's Liberty Leading the People merged revolutionary politics with mythological symbolism. Gothic revival architecture echoed medieval mysticism, and poets like Wordsworth saw nature as divine revelation. The movement validated subjective experience over Enlightenment rationality, paving way for modern psychology in art.

Key Artists: J.M.W. Turner, Eugène Delacroix

Impressionism

Impressionism

19th Century

Light · Color · Modern Life

Rebelling against Academic salons, Impressionists captured fleeting light effects through plein air painting and broken color. Monet's Impression, Sunrise (giving the movement its name) used rapid brushstrokes to depict Le Havre's harbor mists. Degas' ballet dancers revealed modernity's transience, while Renoir's Luncheon of the Boating Party celebrated bourgeois leisure. Scientific color theories by Chevreul influenced pointillist techniques. The portable tin paint tube enabled outdoor work, documenting Paris' transformed boulevards under Haussmann. Though initially mocked, their optical experiments revolutionized perceptual painting.

Key Artists: Claude Monet, Pierre-Auguste Renoir

Modern Art

Modern Art

20th Century

Abstraction · Experimentation · Conceptual

Shattering traditional representation, modernists explored new visual languages. Picasso's Les Demoiselles d'Avignon introduced Cubism's fragmented perspectives, while Kandinsky's color symphonies pioneered pure abstraction. Dadaists like Duchamp challenged art's definition with readymades such as Fountain. Abstract Expressionists (Pollock's drip paintings) emphasized process over product. Bauhaus merged art with industrial design, and Warhol's Pop Art critiqued consumer culture. Movements from Surrealism to Minimalism reflected rapid technological changes and post-war existential anxieties, expanding art's boundaries beyond physical objects.

Key Artists:

Pablo Picasso, Jackson Pollock
